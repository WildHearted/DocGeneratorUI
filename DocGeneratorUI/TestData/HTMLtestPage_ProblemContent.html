Background and context

The Network and Communications Outsourcing market is evolving and expanding rapidly. It is currently a $10B USD market that is growing at 9% year over year (YoY), according to IDC. The market growth is being driven by enterprise CIOs who have decided to outsource the day-to-day management and support of their network and communications services to a trusted third party. This eliminates their need to invest (and continue to invest) in management tools and processes, hiring and retaining networking and communications professionals, and the constant demand to update and reconfigure their technology in response to new business requirements. In addition, there is a growing trend for enterprises to buy new technology in an OPEX model via a managed private or hybrid private / public cloud - thus avoiding the need to use precious, hard to obtain capital on new networking and communications hardware and systems.

As such, the network and communications outsourcing market is evolving to include managed private and hybrid cloud solutions as a key part of their service portfolio. This is truly revolutionising the market and forcing equipment manufacturers to fundamentally alter their strategies. The TSIA (Technology Services Industry Association) reports that 80% of equipment manufacturers had their revenue decline year over year in 2014.

The support challenges are especially true in the communications and networking space, where many enterprises have older, more problematic multi-vendor infrastructures to manage. CIOs and their leadership teams are struggling to operate these environments from a total cost of ownership and performance standpoint. In addition, with capital tight at most companies, the appeal of buying a service via a cloud OPEX service model when transforming and / or upgrading their IT infrastructure has significant appeal. This appeal includes a reduction in total cost of ownership, mitigation of risk, and access to the skills to integrate and support new, emerging technologies that integrate networking, communications, and collaboration technologies and solutions into an enterprise-grade service solution.

Service offering

Our MSEN will offer our clients a portfolio of enterprise-grade managed network service offerings. Each will be offered with unit-based pricing, for the networking outsourcing and managed service market that will enable Dimension Data to take over the day-to-day production operations for all, or a portion, of a client's environment either 'as is' or as part of a new technology transformation. Our service offerings will support multivendor technologies both current and legacy systems, which will be defined in the individual technology service descriptions. We will constantly update and evolve our technology support to stay current with market leaders and incorporate strategic partner technologies and solutions into our portfolio offer.

Our MSEN will provide full ITIL support, inclusive of performance, availability, and problem management for the network infrastructure. We will back these services with outcome-based SLAs to include financial remediation. Any financial remediation shall be based on approved criteria and requirements.

The services will be delivered via a three-tier global delivery model, with a target of 60% to 70% of the FTE requirements for a client engagement being supported by a low-cost delivery centre. The service delivery will also include dedicated or designated 'client intimate' L3 lead engineers who drive production operations support, including ownership of proactive performance, problem, and availability management. Our MSEN must deliver proactive output from these key service delivery areas, where tasks are generated by the ITIL-based service systems rather than being reactive to a client-generated process or raised issue. As such, our services delivery team needs to be supported by an advanced systems management platform, with the objective of using industry leading autonomics to automate as much of the service delivery, especially incident management, as possible.

Finally, an integral part of our service delivery and value to the client is a senior transformation architect, who is an expert in technology, has deep experience with enterprise service delivery, and understands the complexities of deploying new services without causing disruption in a client's business. They will be responsible for driving the transformation plan and the execution of taking the client from current state to the contracted state, working with the delivery L3 engineer.

Delivery model

To maximise cost efficiencies and improve delivery, a 3-tier delivery model is required. A 3-tiered model organises labour into three separate cost and capability segments and is designed to provide low cost remote resources where appropriate and higher cost local resources when needed to satisfy the scope or the demands of the client.

  • Our Central Delivery Organisation (CDO) is located in Bangalore and provides the first tier of support. The objective is to have 60% to 70% of the FTE staffing provided by the CDO.

  • The second tier of support is provided by our regional Global Service Centres (GSC) located in geographic proximity to the client, typically within the host Dimension Data operating region. If the client has a decentralised support model, there may be more than one GSC engaged to deliver the service. This tier of support is best suited for work that requires closer proximity to the client (e.g. Time zone sensitivity specialised technical expertise, language/customs, statutory or regulatory requirements). It is critical that the systems, processes, tools, and client deliverables for the service be identical across all GSCs. This is required to ensure service consistency for our emerging, multi-national Enterprise Services clients, who will expect consistent global service delivery. Moreover, this will also enable all regions to engage and maximise the use of the CDO against a common service delivery model.

  • The third tier of support consists of local or dedicated resources, who are typically located at the client site or at nearby Dimension Data offices. This tier of support is best suited for work that requires a touch or is complex in nature and requires close cooperation or intimacy with the client operations.

This model can be represented as a pyramid whereby the first tier of support (the CDO) serves as the front line (which could involve client interaction)that passes work that it cannot complete to the second tier of support - the GSC. If the GSC cannot complete the work, it is passed to the third tier of support local resources who are responsible for completion of the work and closure of the support event. The 3-tier delivery model is critical to achieving transaction optimisation as work is initially presented to the lowest cost delivery location and moves on to higher cost delivery locations as the complexity or impact/urgency escalates. It is expected that there will be on-going service development work aimed at continuously finding new ways to migrate work from local to GSC to CDO over time. This process needs to be viewed as not only from an overall service perspective, but also from a client-by-client perspective as client immaturity at the outset of an engagement may inhibit the movement of work.

This model provides a key competitive differentiator compared to the Indian outsourcers, who have historically operated in only a two-tiered model, on site resources combined with remote Indian resources. While other competitors are introducing a third tier with regional delivery centres, they are more of an add-on than a change to their core service delivery model.

The MSEN portfolio supports the technology features and service functionality of all in-scope technology vendors. For security appliances, routing and/or switch enabled security services, VoIP services, or any applications running on on-board server instances, the above applies only to the hardware, software, and application(s) to the extent that it is a data forwarding device (packets are either traversing or not traversing the product). Wide Area Network Reachability SLA

Wide Area Data Network Reachability is measured on a calendar month basis and is calculated based on the average of the availability of all sites managed by Dimension Data.

The availability for each site in a reporting period is calculated as follows:

WAN Reachability = (total hours hours of downtime + hours of pre-planned scheduled outages + hours of excused outages) / total hours.

To monitor the reachability of devices, 4-minute polling intervals are used. Two consecutive unreachable polls (all ICMP requests lost) will generate a down condition. Site reachability is determined by polling a single IP address.


The SLA metric will be mutually agreed to by both Dimension Data and client and base lined over the first three months of engagement whether it is achievable.

Excluded from calculations:

  • Voice (PBX) circuit outages

  • Outage duration when site access is not available (for example, site closed)

  • Application availability

  • Scheduled downtime due to preventive maintenance, upgrades, or pre-planned outages mutually agreed by the client and Dimension data

  • Downtime caused by:

    • Action or inaction of the client

    • Unavailability of out-of-band access at the site

    • Virus, worm, distributed denial of service, or any other malicious activity

    • Force majeure events

    • Data circuits not being service managed by Dimension Data

    • Power failure for equipment not protected by UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply)

    • UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply) equipment failure due to malfunction or extended utility outage (battery expires)

    • Site power failures

    • Time for hardware maintenance vendor to respond, as specified in client maintenance agreement

    • Time for carrier service provider to restore service, as specified in client's carrier agreement

    • Client site contact not being available

Site Specific Reachability SLA

Data Network High Availability Site SLA is applicable to those sites where redundant network paths and associated redundant wide-area network carrier services and routers have been provisioned.

Site Availability is calculated as follows:
Site Availability = (total hours hours of downtime + hours of pre-planned scheduled outages + hours of excused outages) / total hours.

For each site that requires High Availability support, the sites equipment configuration and WAN service transport design must meet Dimension Datas engineering and support guidelines to qualify for the SLA. Site Availability is measured as the time that a site is available to transmit and receive data from other Dimension Data managed sites.
